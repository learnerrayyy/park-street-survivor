Week 01

Project Brief & Team Formation

First meeting with the brief — choosing our concept and locking in roles.

DONE +

📅 Week 1  ·  [Date Placeholder]  ·  Group 7 · All Members

In our first lab session, we came together as a team and received the Park Street Survivor brief for the first time. The challenge was clear: design and build a browser-based game that tells a meaningful story — constrained to HTML5 Canvas, a 12-week timeline, and five people with very different skills.

We spent the session mapping out what each person brings to the table and debating possible game concepts. Three ideas competed: a physics-based slope challenge, a point-and-click narrative, and a rhythm game. By the end of the session, we had aligned on the slope mechanic — but with the poetry layer that would become Park Street Survivor's defining feature.

"The brief asked us to design a game that could live in a browser and tell a meaningful story. We left with something more specific: hope, feathers, and a very steep hill."

Deliverables

  • Team contract signed by all members
  • Initial concept brief (500 words)
  • Role assignment document
  • Jira project board created & initial epics populated
Week 02

Game Design Document Submission

Formal GDD submitted — mechanics, narrative arc, visual direction, architecture plan.

DONE +

📅 Week 2  ·  [Date Placeholder]  ·  Group 7 · All Members

The Game Design Document was our most important early deliverable — the document that forces you to stop saying "something like this" and commit to specifics. We split writing responsibilities by domain: Charlotte wrote the technical architecture section, Keyu authored the narrative structure and dialogue trigger plan, Kangrui detailed the level layout, and Lucca defined the visual style with reference boards.

The hardest decision was the core game loop. We had to define exactly how the physics, narrative, and art style would interact in a single run. The result was the "Slope-Poem Loop": physical struggle on the hill surfaces poetic fragments, which in turn motivate continued play.

"The GDD forced us to answer uncomfortable questions early — which meant we could argue about design on paper instead of in code."

Deliverables

  • GDD v1.0 (complete document)
  • Core gameplay loop diagram
  • Mood board & visual style references
  • Sprint 1 backlog populated in Jira (18 tickets)
Week 03

Prototype Sprint 1 Review

First playable build shown — peer feedback collected and fed into Sprint 2.

DONE +

📅 Week 3  ·  [Date Placeholder]  ·  Group 7 · All Members

Presenting the Sprint 1 prototype was a humbling experience. The state machine worked, movement existed, and the first poetic line appeared on-screen — but the physics felt wrong. The slope was either too slippery or too sticky, and the HUD was placeholder text in monospace. Showing unfinished work is part of the process, and feedback from other groups was direct and useful.

Key feedback: the difficulty spike in the first 10 seconds was too harsh, the narrative text disappeared too quickly to read, and the visual palette was not yet distinctive enough. All three became Sprint 2 priorities, logged as high-priority Jira tickets before the session ended.

"Hearing 'I didn't understand what I was supposed to do' from a peer is exactly the kind of signal you can't get from your own team."

Deliverables

  • Playable prototype v0.1 (browser-hosted)
  • Sprint 1 retrospective notes
  • Peer feedback summary (3 groups)
  • Sprint 2 planning — updated Jira backlog
Week 04

User Testing & Feedback Integration

Structured playtesting with external testers — usability issues logged and prioritised.

IN PROGRESS +

📅 Week 4  ·  [Date Placeholder]  ·  Group 7 · Charlotte · Layla

For this session we brought in five external testers — people outside the programme with no prior knowledge of the game. We used a think-aloud protocol: testers spoke their thoughts while playing, and two team members observed and took notes without intervening.

The most striking finding: four of five testers quit before the first poetry trigger fired, because the opening difficulty curve was too steep. We also discovered that the tutorial — which we assumed was self-evident from the controls — was invisible to anyone who hadn't built the game themselves. Both findings reshaped Sprint 2 scope significantly.

"Watching someone fail in silence at something you thought was obvious is the fastest way to learn what your game is actually communicating."

Deliverables

  • User testing plan & moderation scripts
  • Session observation notes (5 testers)
  • Prioritised issue log with severity ratings
  • GDD v2.0 — updated with test findings
